Lebanese baking is a rich and flavorful tradition that reflects the country's diverse culinary heritage. From aromatic za'atar bread to delicate pastries like baklava, Lebanese baking showcases a blend of Middle Eastern flavors and influences. Staples like pita bread and manoushe are not just daily sustenance but cultural icons, enjoyed across Lebanon and beyond. Techniques passed down through generations ensure that each creation is imbued with history and craftsmanship, making Lebanese baking not just a culinary art but a celebration of community and heritage.

mamoul history

In the serene village of Barouk, Elias, a skilled baker, poured his heart into crafting Mamoul cookies shaped like snowflakes. With each delicate shape, he sought to capture the beauty of the mountains that Layla, his childhood friend and secret love, adored so dearly. Using white sugar to mimic the pristine snow, Elias hoped to express his feelings for Layla in a language as pure as the winter landscape. When Layla tasted the snow-shaped treats, she was captivated by Elias's artistry and touched by his silent declaration of love for her and their shared homeland. And so, in the quiet of the mountain village, Elias's Mamoul cookies became a sweet symbol of his affection for Layla and the majestic mountains they both cherished.Now mamool is shared in all happy moments and celebration


Raw Meat


One day in a small village in lebanon found it self, in the face of the encroaching colonial army, the villagers found themselves cut off from their usual sources of sustenance. With dwindling supplies and no means to kindle fires, they turned to their ancestral knowledge, recalling stories of survival during times of strife. Raw meat, seasoned with fragrant spices and shared among them, became not just a meal, but a declaration of their resilience and unity. Each bite served as a reminder that even in the darkest of times, their spirit could not be extinguished.
